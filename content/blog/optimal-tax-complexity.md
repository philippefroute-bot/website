---
title: "Is Tax Complexity Always Bad? A View from Law and Economics"
date: 2024-11-10
summary: "Tax codes are routinely criticised for their complexity. But complexity is not always inefficient — it can serve important functions in screening taxpayers and enabling fine-grained redistribution."
---

Tax codes are routinely criticised for their complexity. Legislators, think-tanks, and international organisations regularly call for simplification, citing compliance costs and the erosion of taxpayer trust. This view is not wrong, but it is incomplete.

## Complexity as a screening device

From a mechanism-design perspective, complexity can serve a useful function: it screens taxpayers according to characteristics that the tax authority cannot directly observe. A complex system with many deductions, thresholds, and special provisions forces taxpayers to sort themselves, revealing information about income sources, family structure, or risk tolerance that a simple flat rate cannot capture.

This is not merely a theoretical observation. The literature on optimal nonlinear taxation — from Mirrlees (1971) onwards — shows that the tax schedule should in principle be tailored to the full distribution of types. Complexity is one imperfect way to achieve this when types are multidimensional.

## The cost side

Of course, complexity is not free. Compliance costs are real: time spent on tax returns, fees paid to accountants, and errors that generate disputes and litigation. There is also a distributional concern — sophisticated taxpayers (and those who can afford good advisers) navigate complexity more effectively than unsophisticated ones, potentially inverting the redistributive intent of the system.

## A more nuanced policy debate

The upshot is that the case for tax simplification is strong in some contexts (reducing compliance costs for low-income filers, eliminating provisions that exist only due to lobbying) and weak in others (provisions that encode genuine economic distinctions). A good tax reform needs to distinguish between *useful* and *wasteful* complexity — a distinction that the slogans of simplification advocates often blur.

This is an area where law and economics has much to contribute: legal rules and economic incentives interact precisely through the structure of the tax code, and a purely economic or purely legal analysis will miss important dimensions.
