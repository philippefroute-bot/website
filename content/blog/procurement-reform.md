Transparency reforms in public procurement have proliferated over the past decade. Motivated by concerns about corruption, inefficiency, and the misallocation of public resources, governments across Europe and beyond have introduced e-procurement platforms, mandatory publication of tender documents, and open contracting standards.

The intuition behind these reforms is compelling: sunlight is the best disinfectant. But the evidence is more nuanced.

## What transparency can and cannot do

Transparency reduces the rents available to colluding bidders by making outcomes observable. When contract awards are published, losing bidders can detect patterns of favouritism and either complain through legal channels or adjust their own bidding behaviour. This competitive discipline is real and has been documented in several settings.

However, transparency can also backfire. If bid prices are disclosed, this provides focal points that facilitate coordination among bidders. Several empirical studies — including work by Conley and Decarolis (2016) on Italian procurement — find that transparency *increases* collusion in some market structures. The mechanism is straightforward: easy communication is not required if prices are publicly posted.

## Implications for reform design

The lesson for policymakers is that the design of transparency matters as much as its presence. Disclosing winning bids after award is generally beneficial; disclosing all bids in real time is not. Opening datasets to researchers (with appropriate privacy protections) creates accountability without creating collusion templates.

This is a domain where careful empirical work — ideally using administrative data and credible identification strategies — can directly inform policy design. The stakes are high: public procurement represents 10–15% of GDP in most OECD countries.
